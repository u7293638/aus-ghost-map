IS HE THE RIGHT MAN? BY THE AUTHOR OF "BEWITCHED," "ARISEN," &c.;, &c.; (From the Penny Miscellany.) CHAPTER XLVIII. A GENTLEMAN HERDSMAN. Look here upon this picture and on this. The counterfeit presentment of two brothers. , The flash and outbreak of a fiery mind, A savageness in unreclaimed blood." Hamlet. If the reader would know how ex- asperatingly cheerful and comforting a well-spread table and a row of knives and forks can look, let him traverse an Australian wilderness for a given number of nights and days, subsisting during the journey on two small birds, half cooked, and on fresh air. Both Cuthbert and Guy's eyes gladdened at sight of the food, and hastily accepting the jovial Dutchman's request to set to and "begin," in the light of a command, they seized knives and forks, and commenced filling the vacuums which their journey and fast- ing had created, not in the least disturbed by the sudden clatter of horses' hoofs, and the subsequent entry of three strongly built men, who tramped into the room, and regarding the two travellers with a single stare of curiosity, grunted a salutation to the farmer, and took possession, with solemn business-like gravity, of the re- maining places ; save the one at the head of the table, into which the Dutchman dropped, and shouting out, "Yi! hi!" with which two monosyllables he usually prefaced his conversation, he addressed the tallest of the three new comers with "Hi! yi! Long Pen, what is to run?" meaning, how far had the cattle wan- dered from the settlement? Long Ben, without raising his eyes from his plate, and gulping down a piece of meat about the size of a horse- shoe, at the great risk of suffocation, growled, ''Nigh upon twenty mile." "Tweenty mile! Hi, yi! what the tevil—Harree, vare is tem short tails gon,' the tam—" "Cross hills," responded the next shepherd, without waiting for the re- mainder of the sentence, "and I'm— if I'm going arter 'em to- night." "Hi, yi! you must, you shall !" gurgled the Dutchman, waving his knife with a comprehensive sweep. "What the devil—" ''More taters!" roared shepherd number three, and reaching over the table as he spoke, he seized the salt, up- setting in the passage a jug of beer over Guy's plate. "Hi yi!" roared the Dutchman, dashing his knife into the table, and waving his fork about. "tare goes te beer! tam—" "Moor beer!" roared Long Ben, catching up the jug with promptness, and, first emptying the remainder of the liquor down his bronzed throat, throw- ing it, with an emphatic oath to a good- looking girl, who stood by, evidently expecting to receive it. Cuthbert started with astonishment. Had he reached the end of the world, and gained the last dregs of civilisation, or, was he dreaming? And Guy— he seemed not in the least surprised or moved by the savage manners, voices and general tumult, but ate on, having snatched at another plate, and refilled it from the common dish with the cool- est of unconcern, as if he had eaten at the same table and amongst the same men all his life! Overcoming his as- tonishment, he determined, as far as he was able, to do in Rome what the Ro- mans did; so, thrusting his knife in a potatoe he conveyed to his plate, and snatching up the jug of beer directly the girl set it on the table, he filled his horn mug, draining it at a draught, and returning it to the table with a slap. Guy looked up sharply, with a smile of approval, and Cuthbert who guessed that he was playing a part, put on as rough and savage a manner as possible, which so far deceived the Dutchman, who had begun to be rather suspicious of the new so-called shepherds.
