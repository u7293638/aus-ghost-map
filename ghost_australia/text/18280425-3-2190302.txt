HURRICANE IN THE WEST INDIES. ANTIGUA, AUG. 21. It has pleased the Almighty Disposer of Events, in the manifestation of his divine wrath, and in the fullness of his wisdom, ma- jesty, and power, to visit this island with a most violent and destructive storm, which took place on the morning of Friday last. Those only who witnessed the great hurri- cane of 1772, recollect any thing which re- sembles the ravage it has made throughout every district of the country. Early on Thursday the wind was perceived to veer from its usual tropical direction to the north, and frequent puffs, with a gloomy atmos- phere, indicated symptoms of a change of weather for the worse, which, however, did not create much alarm. The evening set in darkly, and it blew very hard during the night, with frequent showers. At day- light the violence of the wind appeared to increase, although up to this period it had done but trifling damage; but as the morning ad- vanced, it came round to N. E. and then to E. and between seven and ten o'clock it rose to a tempest, and raged with tremendous fury, levelling every thing of feeble resistance, and severely injuring others whose weight or strength prevented their entire destruction. Great numbers of large full-grown trees were torn up, and thrown to a considerable dis- tance; and heavy bodies, such as carts, the leaden gutters of houses, &c.; whirled from their places in every capricious direction. Many of the larger buildings in town suffered more or less damage; while the smaller and less substantial habitations have, in some in- stances, been entirely demolished, and in others rendered so dilapidated, as to afford but a precarious shelter to the needy and unfortunate inmates. From every part of the island we have accounts of the most gloomy description. Every plantation has suffered, and some to a considerable extent, in sugar- works, dwelling and out-house, &c.; The provision crops scarcely retain a vestige of verdure, from being seered by the wind; and the cane-fields, which showed the highest bloom and promise, now have the appearance of guinea-grass in dry weather. The public works at the Ridge and its vicinity have like- wise suffered most severely and extensively, but we feel pleasure in stating that we have not heard of any lives being lost in that quarter. The harbour of St. John being more ex- posed to a north-west wind, the shipping did not experience so much of the storm as they have done at former periods. The schooner Robert Grant and sloop Sophia were upset at their moorings; and we are concerned to say, that two men belonging to the former lost their lives. A valuable boatman also lost his life in attempting to secure his craft from the fury of the elements. The brig Strabe, of Glasgow; schooner Eliza, of Prince Edward's Island; sloop Venilia, of Bermuda; and ten other vessels belonging to the port, rode out the storm. The schooner Diligence, of Halifax; sloop Catherine, of Tortola ; a French sloop from Guadaloupe; and eight of our own vessels went ashore. Some of these, however, are already afloat, and others are likely to be so shortly. But of the various misfortunes which have befallen us, we are compelled to single out the soli- tary and lamentable case of the loss of the schooner Thistle, belonging to Mr. Sanne McCarty, which sailed on Tuesday for Old Road and other .... in that direction, with a full freight of dry goods, &c.; the pro- perty of various persons, as well as the per- sonal effects of Mr. Robins (Moravian Mis- sionary), all of which are totally lost. It is not long since Mr. McCarty lost a valuable drogher, which was the principal support of a numerous family entirely dependent on the exertions of a father. The present is indeed a calamity to be deplored, and we sincerely trust it may receive the attention which it so imperiously demands. The day of this awful visitation exhibited a picture of desolation not easily described. At its close his Excellency the Governor took the benevolent precaution of ordering a patrol of the West India Regiment, which paraded the streets all that night, for the protection of property, whose fences and enclosures being blown down, exposed them to the plunder of thieves, with whom these occurences are considered as a ju.....
