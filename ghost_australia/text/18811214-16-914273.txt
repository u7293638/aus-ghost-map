THEIR FIGHTING QUALITIES. They are far from using any delusive trickery or, circumventing sleight against man or other animal, either in self-support of self-defence such as the bird practises in safeguard of her nest, or the angling fishes in their capture of prey much less do they display that innate spite against even their inveterate persecute which authors describe and teachers keep all out amid the stagnant scum of prejudice. If they strike the hand that by mis- chance approaches them, or the fact that presses them unwittingly, they do so not in malice prepense, nor even in revenge these are passions too human-but simply in self-protection. If they fight, it is for dear life not for the hereditary sentiment of a clan. But, in truth, they fight not at all they are non- combatants for very cowardice. Stories of pertinacious aggressive- ness on the part of snakes are not uncommon, but, as those who habitually kill or capture Snakes of all kinds unhesitatingly affirm that they exhibit the purest anxiety to escape, these apparent instances of combativeness would doubtless be susceptible of a different interpretation were all the circumstances known. An incident that of late occurred to the writer may possibly be an explanatory type of all such cases. Seeing a large black snake in motion on the edge of a lagoon, between the water and a tree, he intercepted it to secure so fine a speci- men. To his surprise the snake, instead of retreating, made a determined rush towards him. For several minutes, wheeling right and left, he made a show of fight, fiercely evading the blows aimed at him at close quarters, till at length, with a sudden dash that brought him in contact with his assailant's person, he disappeared in the rear. He had made good his escape into his hole at the root of the tree, and all his seeming ferocity was but a nervous anxiety to reach it. To comprehend the modes of locomotion natural to snakes, some points of their structure must necessarily be understood. The snake's backbone is elongated by virtue of the excessive multiplication of its jointed segments or verbal to. Closely interlocked by projecting keys, and firmly bound together by rough tissue, these bones have but limited motion one upon another their greatest play, that from side to side, is not extensive ; from behind forwards it is much less ; in a backward direction it is scarcely appreciable ; while any degree of revolution round their centres of contact is impossible. But the mobility of each, though restricted when multiplied by the whole number of the series, which may amount to upwards of 300, confers on the spine its incomparable facility of lateral and couvoluting motion. Along the upper surface of the back, on each side of the spine, runs an enormous mass of muscle, while the under surface of the spine is supplied with another body of muscle not usually of much power. These "dorsal" muscles are the agents of all secure and erection of the body. The disposition of the upper muscle causes the greater part of its bulk to act laterally ; if the muscle of one side alone contract the effect is motion towards that side from simple bending to the complex coil ; if different portions act alternately on opposite sides the body is thrown into folds- certain layers may, under strong volition, jerk it backward with a slight flexure. It is inopportune to enter upon their peculiarities. Let it suffice that the motion chiefly impressed upon the ribs in an oscillation parallel with the spine, whereby the lower ends and shield connected with them move through a small space at each pull of their muscles. Between the end of the rib and the outer edge of the shield is a slip of muscle which, aided by another yet smaller between the Shields themselves, raises that edge synchronously with the advance of the rib. The erected edge of the shield catching on any asperity of the surface beneath it becomes a fulcrum, enabling the muscles of the rib behind it to draw the body forward, and every scale in contact with the surface being put into action successively the whole body is carried onwards by a perpetual series of minute steps. Were it not that " there are more things in heaven and earth than are dreamt of in our philosophy" we might dogmatise on and declare it to be ridiculously impossible that the eye of a snake could have any influence whatever over another animal-much less that it could drag down into the jaw below it the very bird on the wing. To this length we will not go, but endeavour to form a fair judgment of the degree and kind of truth open to us. Reports of something taken for a fascinating process, and that by men whose intelligence is to the writer's knowledge sober enough to accredit their opinion, are too many to be lightly laid aside. We must accept as a fact that snakes do occasionally obtain food in an extraordinary way by means that are at least suggestive of mal influence. The most frequent cases are of birds seen to flutter nervously to and fro before the gazing reptile all the while uttering anxious cries, as though conscious of a power which was subduing them, drawing nearer and nearer at every turn, till they are brought within the range of their destroyer, or helplessly drop into his open jaws. Other instances are of frogs which have been seen to sit as though in lethargy while the snake has been creeping slowly upon them till it could feel them with its very tongue. There are two or more theories about such cases, One that the victims are subjected to an influence, unknown in kind peculiar to snakes, and exercised by them consciously or unconsciously a power which by unaccountable means paralyses the voluntary muscles, or overbears volition and gives it a self destructive tendency. Another is that the observed actions or inaction of the animal are due to a perfectly natural cause-the con- fusing or paralysing effect of terror.
