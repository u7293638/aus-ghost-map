THE FRENCH AND GERMAN WOUNDED. The special correspondent of the Telegraph, writing from the German head quarters at Versailles on the 17th December, says -sad accounts of the state of the French and German wounded in Bcnugency have been received A great many of the men injured in the battles between the Grand Duke and Gliniuv have positively died of hunger Not a few were brought into Beaugency to expire, who had been 1) ing for three days and nights in the blood, without a particle of food to eat or a drop of water to drink Edibles of any sort are not to be bought for money in Beaugency or in the villages near that town One hundred and sixty severely wounded men are lying in the cell of an Ursuline come out there-as many more in the tiny provincial theatre Every house contains from six to a dozen or more sufferers, but there is only a single surgeon to attend to six or seven hundred cases The stench in some of the houses in horrible, for fester and gangrene are raging, and many of the men are in a state of partial decomposition before they give up the ghost 'The little town was shelled for a short time, as you are aware and I will take leave to relate one inexpressibly sad episode of the bombardment A French gentleman of the neighbourhood-a truer servant of the Cross than many that hear it on their arms had volunteered his services as a bearer and adjourned During the action he had been busy in the field, bringing in under heavy fire wounded men of both nations , amongst others a Prussian soldier whom he attended before the town was captured, in an ex tempore hospital, established in a private house. He was standing by the bed of his suffering enemy, ministering to his wants, when a shell struck the house, penetrated the wall of the room in which his ward was situated, and hurst, not injuring any of the patients, but carrying off both Houses of the good Frenchman, who fell, never to use again. The wounded Prussian managed to get out of bed, though badly hurt, and endeavoured to staunch the blood of his benefactor, over whose dead body he was subsequently found, sobbing like a little child Could there be a more glorious death ''
