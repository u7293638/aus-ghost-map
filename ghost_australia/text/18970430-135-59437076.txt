THE DeY. He led her home, home to the House of Blood. . Through the pine trees they went to it, hand in hand. The poppies waved no more amongst the yellow corn, for it had been cut down, and the poppies with it. The small red pimpernels had vanished in the first Autumn breath, and the sun set at a different angle. The windows of the house no longer dripped with blood at sunset. He had led her home. If eudden hor ror seized him at a quick relapse into his old habit of thought, a small white hand would touth him, and the sun shine would break from his eyes, and the shadows fly. One kiss had made amends for all the agony of years. Old Foley watched them, and chuckled over them. He had known it all along, the bad old man. Perhaps his master might have looked higher for a lassie with more wealth and station, but he could not have found a bonnier one in the length and breadth of the land. Why was it that the country folk saw no more ghosts from the day she came ? Why were they not afraid to walk in twilight hours through the haunted house 7 Had one bright young face dispelled the silent horrors of the house in such a little time ? It went up and down the gloomy passages, and into the empty rooms, until a change came upon the place, and the gloom and emptiness fled away. One year became another, and the legends of the house were forgotten by everyone but old Foley. He used to matter on and on about the things he had seen, when the rooms had been left in darkness and dirt. He told of bony fingers protruding through the armor in the ball, and of grinning skulls within the helmets. Racy's roundeyed chil dren listened to him, but they thought be spoke of ages ago, when there were fairies as well as ghosts in the world. Other people said he doted. Little Winnie gathered a large bunch of scarlet poppies. She brought them in and scattered them all over her father and mother. Racy laughed.
