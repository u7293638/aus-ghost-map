A Broken Leg.— On Friday last, Henry Watson, an employe of Mr John Fawns, met with an accident, by which he incurred a fracture of the thigh. He was assisting to lift a half hogshead of ale from a dray on to the wharf, when the cask slipped, falling upon the unfortunate man's leg. Watson was taken home, and was attended by Dr. Mason, who reduced the fracture, and placed the wounded limb in splints. The man is re ported to be progressing favorably. Another Child Burnt to Death. — The frequency of accidents to women and chil dren throughout the colonies, resulting in death, by that most terrible of all means of expelling life— fire— is really appalling. Only last Sunday a poor infirm old woman, age'l 71 years, was accidentally burnt to death at Young Town, and on Thursday, 13th, a fino healthy girl, about six years old, the daugh ter of Mr Lamb, one of the employes at the British and Tasnmninn Iron Company's works, at PortLempriere, wasalso accidentally burnt to death. The child had been out playing on the beach and set fire to some tussocks of grass. The flames from these caught her frock, and the poor child was literally burnt to death before her scrcmns attracted others to the terrible scene. Were Australian statisticians to set apart a column showing the number of deaths resulting from burning, the total in one year would scarcely be credited. It is almost impossible to take up any colonial newspaper without finding the details of the death by fire oE some help less victim— generally a helpless woman or an innocent child ; and yet it is difficult to suggest any useful practical preventive measure for the purpose of lessening the number of these terrible occurrences.
